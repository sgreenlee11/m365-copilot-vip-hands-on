From signal to executive action

Executive field briefing

One business day. Six Copilot moves.

Instead of practicing six versions of summarization, this lab follows one executive decision from first signal to final communication.

The challenge

Regional performance has softened while customer comments point to product availability and service friction. Your leadership meeting begins shortly. What changed, what matters, and what should the organization do next?

AskCatch up across your work and identify the signal.
DecideInterrogate data and turn evidence into a recommendation.
ActCreate the message and capture accountable follow-through.

Activity 2 · 5 minutes

Prompt for a decision—not a paragraph.

Give Copilot a goal, the context that changes the answer, the source of truth, and the shape of a useful response.

GoalWhat outcome do you need?
ContextWhat should influence the answer?
SourceWhere should Copilot look?
ExpectationsWhat format and level of detail?
EvidenceWhat facts support the conclusion?
JudgmentWhat must you verify or decide?

Executive prompt pattern

Help me decide [the decision]. The situation is [relevant context and constraints]. Use [specific files, messages, meetings, or data] as the source. Identify the strongest evidence, competing interpretations, risks, and missing information. Recommend a course of action and format it as [desired output]. Clearly distinguish facts from assumptions.

Facilitator move: Ask participants which phrase makes this prompt safer and more useful than “summarize this.”

Activity 3 · 7 minutes

Catch up: find the signal.

Use Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat to synthesize recent work context, then narrow the response to decisions and risks.

Copilot ChatWork contentExecutive triage
  1. Set your scope. Replace the bracketed areas with the functions or stakeholders you own.
  2. Run the prompt. Review the sources Copilot provides and open at least one supporting item.
  3. Challenge the result. Ask what is uncertain, contradictory, or missing.

Leadership signal scan

Review my work from the past five business days related to [operations, customers, products, people, finance, or another area]. Identify the five developments most likely to require executive attention. For each, explain what changed, why it matters now, the decision or intervention that may be needed, and the source supporting the finding. Separate confirmed facts from possible implications.

Pressure-test follow-up

Which of these findings has the weakest evidence or conflicting signals? Show me what I should verify before acting.

Reflection: Did Copilot surface a decision, or merely organize information?

Activity 4 · 7 minutes

Prepare: enter informed.

Use Copilot with an upcoming meeting and the content available to you in Teams or Outlook. The goal is not a generic agenda—it is a sharper leadership posture.

Teams or OutlookMeeting contextQuestions and tensions
  1. Choose a real upcoming meeting with enough existing context to make the exercise useful.
  2. Use Copilot in the meeting workflow to review relevant context available to you.
  3. Verify participant positions before treating them as fact.

Leadership meeting pre-read

Prepare me for [meeting name]. Summarize the purpose, recent developments connected to the agenda, and any decisions that appear to be needed. Identify the participants whose input will be important, but do not infer a person's position unless it is supported by available content. End with three questions I should ask and two assumptions the group should test.

Engagement moment: Pair up and compare the questions Copilot proposed. Which one would materially improve the meeting?

Analyze this performance data to determine where leadership attention would have the greatest impact this week. Activity 6 · 8 minutes

Create: shape the message.

The analysis is not the deliverable. Turn it into communication that fits the audience and the moment.

Word

Draft a one-page executive decision brief from the analysis.

PowerPoint

Create a short leadership narrative or slide outline using the source material.

Outlook

Draft a concise request for alignment or approval.

Teams

Translate the decision into a clear leadership update.

Executive decision brief

Using the analysis and source material, draft a one-page executive decision brief. Use the sections Situation, Evidence, Options, Recommendation, Risks, and Decision Needed. Keep the tone direct and neutral. Preserve important numbers, cite or identify the source of factual claims, and flag anything that still requires validation.

Audience shift

Now adapt this for [store leaders / the board / associates / a vendor]. Change the level of detail and language without changing the underlying facts or recommendation.

Reflection: What changed when the audience changed—and what should not have changed?

Activity 7 · 6 minutes

Follow through: make it accountable.

Use Copilot in Teams to review a meeting summary or transcript and convert discussion into explicit commitments.

Teams meeting recapDecisionsOwnersOpen questions

Decision and owner record

Using this meeting's available recap or transcript, create a decision and owner record. Include decisions made, action items with the named owner and stated due date, unresolved questions, and items that were discussed but not decided. Do not invent an owner or deadline when one was not stated; mark it as unassigned or not specified.

Shareable version

Rewrite this as a concise Teams post. Put the decision first, then owners and dates, then open questions. End by asking participants to correct anything that does not match their understanding.

Activity 8 · 5 minutes

Lead responsibly.

Copilot accelerates preparation and production. Executive accountability remains with the person who acts on the output.

VerifyOpen sources and confirm names, dates, numbers, and quotations.
SeparateDistinguish facts, interpretations, assumptions, and recommendations.
ProtectUse content only within its intended permissions and business context.
ChallengeAsk for counterarguments, missing evidence, and alternate explanations.
EditApply your judgment to tone, audience, and organizational implications.
OwnThe decision and communication remain yours—not Copilot's.
